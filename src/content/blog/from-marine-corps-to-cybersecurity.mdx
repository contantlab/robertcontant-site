My path into cybersecurity was not a straight line.

I did not grow up planning to work in software, cloud security, governance, risk, compliance, or cybersecurity engineering. For a long time, if you had asked me what I expected to do after the military, I probably would have said something related to law enforcement, public safety, intelligence, or national security. Technology was around me, but I did not yet see it as the center of my professional future.

What I can see clearly now, looking back, is that the thread was always there. I have always been pulled toward how things work, how systems fit together, and how to build something that does a job better than it was being done before. Cybersecurity is where that instinct eventually landed, but the underlying interest is broader than security alone. It is a genuine fascination with technology, IT, and innovation, and with the act of building things. Security just happens to be the part of that world I find most compelling, because it sits at the intersection of technology, business, and trust.

That focus came into view gradually, through a series of experiences that exposed me to how deeply technology, security, business, and trust are connected.

## Marine Corps and embassy duty

After high school, I joined the United States Marine Corps. I initially served as a Signals Intelligence Operator, which gave me an early appreciation for information, communication systems, operational security, and the importance of disciplined analysis. Later in my enlistment, I joined the Marine Corps Embassy Security Guard program and was assigned to Marine Detachments at the U.S. Embassy in London, the U.S. Consulate in Shanghai, and the U.S. Embassy in Sarajevo.

Those years shaped me in ways that still influence how I approach work today. Embassy duty exposed me to international environments, high-trust operations, cross-cultural communication, and the importance of protecting people, facilities, information, and missions. It also broadened my view of the world. I enjoyed my time in Shanghai so much that when my enlistment ended, I decided to return there.

## Stumbling into software, SaaS, and compliance

While living in Shanghai, I worked on completing my bachelor's degree, studied Mandarin Chinese, and taught English to support myself while figuring out the next stage of my career. Toward the end of 2016, I met the founders of Pera, formerly Seedlink, a small startup in Shanghai that was beginning to expand into the European market. They were looking for someone who could manage customer accounts in Europe, and I joined the company at an early and exciting stage.

At the time, I did not realize that role would become my entry point into the world of software, SaaS, privacy, security, and compliance.

As Pera started working with European customers, we began receiving questions that none of us were fully prepared for at first. Were we GDPR compliant? Did we have ISO 27001 certification? How did we protect customer data? What security controls did we have in place?

Like many people in small startups, I became the person in the room who volunteered to figure it out. I started researching GDPR, ISO 27001, security policies, data protection practices, customer questionnaires, and the basics of building a security and compliance program. What began as "let me Google this and see what we need to do" gradually turned into a major part of my job.

That experience taught me something important: security is not just a technical function, and compliance is not just paperwork. At its best, security is about building trust. It is about understanding how a business works, where the risks are, what customers need to feel confident, and how to create systems and processes that protect the organization without slowing it down unnecessarily.

It also taught me something I would only fully appreciate later. The part I enjoyed most was not the paperwork. It was the building. Figuring out how a system should work, then standing it up and watching it run, was the part that kept me up at night in a good way.

## Building security programs from scratch

Eventually, I moved back to the United States with my family. Because Pera was not operating in the U.S. market, I began looking for new opportunities and joined Zoovu in an Information Security Lead and Data Protection Officer type role. Once again, I found myself building a security and compliance program from the ground up, but this time the primary driver was SOC 2 Type 2 rather than ISO 27001.

Within my first year at Zoovu, we successfully obtained the company's first SOC 2 Type 2 report. That was a major milestone and another formative experience. It gave me a clearer view of how SaaS companies are evaluated by customers, auditors, partners, and internal stakeholders. It also helped me understand the difference between building security for the sake of passing an audit and building security because it actually improves the organization.

Over time, that distinction became increasingly important to me.

My early roles at Pera and Zoovu exposed me to technology, IT, SaaS, cybersecurity, privacy, and compliance in a way I had never experienced before. I learned by doing. I built programs from scratch. I worked with engineers, executives, customers, auditors, legal teams, and operations teams. I learned how security questionnaires work, how audits work, how policies and controls are created, and how customer trust is won or lost.

But I also learned something about myself: I was much more interested in the technical and engineering side of the field than I was in traditional GRC, compliance, and audit-driven work.

## Finding the part of the field that energizes me

That realization took time. Early in my career, the boundaries between information security, compliance, privacy, IT, cybersecurity, and risk management were not always clear to me. I knew I was in the general field, but I had not yet fully separated which parts of the field energized me and which parts drained me.

The more experience I gained, the more obvious it became. I enjoyed solving technical problems. I enjoyed understanding how systems worked. I enjoyed incident response, vulnerability management, cloud security, endpoint security, identity and access management, network security, application security, and security operations. I enjoyed building and improving things, and that same instinct shows up everywhere in my life, not just in security. I like automating tedious work, wiring tools together, and turning a messy manual process into something that runs on its own.

What I did not enjoy as much was the recurring cycle of audit preparation, evidence collection, control narratives, spreadsheet management, and compliance work that sometimes felt disconnected from real security outcomes.

That does not mean compliance is unimportant. In fact, my compliance background has made me a better security professional. It taught me how organizations are governed, how risk is communicated, how evidence is evaluated, how executives make decisions, and how customers assess trust. But I eventually realized that I did not want my career to be defined primarily by audits. I wanted to move closer to the work of actually building and securing systems.

In the years that followed, I worked for other B2B SaaS companies as well as larger organizations such as HCLSoftware, where I managed security and compliance projects across a broad portfolio of software solutions, including SaaS products, data centers, and globally distributed teams. Those roles gave me exposure to a much larger scale of operations, more complex environments, and a wide range of customer, regulatory, and technical security obligations.

Today, in my role at XTEL, I continue to work across both cybersecurity and GRC, and I am deliberately moving deeper into the hands-on engineering side. I support areas such as incident response, vulnerability management, external SOC coordination, penetration testing, security operations, risk management, audits, vendor risk, policies, and security program maturity. It is a role that continues to challenge me and gives me opportunities to keep building my technical skills while also applying the broader security and compliance experience I have developed over the past several years.

## Building things outside of work

Outside of work, I have become much more intentional about building hands-on skills, and this is where my broader love of technology really shows.

I have built a home lab and home server running Proxmox, open-source security tools, SIEM tooling, virtual machines, and practice environments. I experiment with Active Directory environments, scripting users and groups, vulnerability scanning, penetration testing concepts, endpoint security, and pfSense for firewalling and network segmentation. I treat my home network as a real networking and security project. I have also been building my own AI infrastructure at home, running local models and experimenting with self-hosted, privacy-respecting automation, because I think the intersection of AI and security is one of the most interesting places to be right now. I also continue to use platforms like TryHackMe and other learning resources, although I now try to be much more structured and deliberate than I was earlier in my journey.

None of this feels like a chore to me. I do it because I genuinely enjoy it. The lab is as much a hobby as it is professional development, and that is probably the clearest signal of where my real interests lie.

That structure matters, though.

One of the biggest lessons I have learned is that effort alone is not enough. Early on, I spent many nights after my kids went to bed working through labs, watching lessons, and trying to absorb as much as I could. At the time, it felt productive. But months later, I sometimes struggled to point to something concrete that proved what I had learned.

That was a wake-up call.

If I were starting over, I would spend more time up front researching the different areas of cybersecurity, compliance, auditing, GRC, DevOps, software engineering, cloud security, security operations, application security, and infrastructure. I would identify which areas genuinely interested me, then build a structured path around the skills needed for those areas. I would focus more on fundamentals earlier: networking, operating systems, cloud platforms, identity, secure software development, scripting, and how modern IT environments actually work.

## Network, build, document

Most importantly, I would document everything.

That is one of the reasons I created this portfolio website. For too long, I worked on projects, built things, researched topics, helped solve problems, and developed skills without consistently documenting them. That was a missed opportunity. In cybersecurity, it is not enough to say you are learning. You need to show your work.

Build the lab. Write the blog post. Push code to GitHub. Document the architecture. Explain what you learned. Share your thought process. Build the project you wish someone would hire you to do. If you cannot get the exact job you want yet, start doing pieces of that job on your own time and make the work visible.

My current view is simple: network, build, document.

Networking matters because people who have already made the transition can help you understand what the work actually looks like. Building matters because hands-on experience is what turns theory into judgment. Documentation matters because it creates proof of your progress and helps others understand how you think.

I am also pursuing a Master's in Cybersecurity. I am grateful to have GI Bill benefits available, and the program gives me a structured way to continue adding to my knowledge. At the same time, I am realistic about the role of degrees. A degree can help. Certifications can help. In some cases, credentials such as CISSP or CISM can help get past filters and signal foundational knowledge. But degrees and certifications alone are rarely the game changer people hope they will be.

The real game changer is applied skill.

Can you explain a concept clearly? Can you troubleshoot a real environment? Can you secure an identity system? Can you read logs and identify what matters? Can you understand a vulnerability and communicate the risk? Can you build a practical process that improves security without creating unnecessary friction? Can you keep learning when the field changes?

Those are the questions I care about now.

## Where I am headed

My journey into cybersecurity has been long, indirect, and sometimes frustrating, but it has also been clarifying. I started in the Marine Corps, moved through embassy security, lived and worked overseas, joined a startup in Shanghai, stumbled into GDPR and ISO 27001, built security programs at SaaS companies, helped achieve SOC 2, worked across global software environments, and gradually discovered that my real professional interest is in cybersecurity: not as an abstract concept, but as hands-on work that protects systems, people, and organizations. Underneath all of it is a broader passion for technology, IT, and innovation, and for building things that work.

I am still on that journey. I am not where I want to be yet, and I do not pretend to have it all figured out. But I know the direction I am moving in.

I want to keep building. I want to keep learning. I want to keep getting closer to the technical work of securing modern systems, and I want to keep following my curiosity wherever the most interesting problems in technology happen to be. I want this website to serve as a record of that process: the projects I build, the lessons I learn, the ideas I explore, and the path I am taking from compliance-heavy security work toward deeper cybersecurity engineering.

This blog is part of that effort.

It is a way to document the journey, share what I am learning, and hopefully connect with others who are trying to make a similar transition. If there is one thing I have learned, it is that cybersecurity, and technology more broadly, is too vast to navigate passively. You have to choose a direction, build real things, document your progress, and keep going.
